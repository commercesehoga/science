1. Electric Charge — Properties

  • Quantization: Charge exists only in integer multiples of e (e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C). q = ±ne. You can never have a charge of 1.5e.
  • Conservation: Total charge of an isolated system is always constant. Charges can be created or destroyed only in equal and opposite pairs (pair production/annihilation).
  • Additive nature: Total charge = algebraic sum of all individual charges. Signs matter.
  • Invariance: Charge of a particle does NOT change with its speed (unlike mass). Charge is Lorentz invariant.

2. Coulomb's Law — In Depth

Force between two point charges q₁ and q₂ separated by distance r:

F = (1/4πε₀) × (q₁q₂/r²) — in vacuum (free space)
F = (1/4πε₀K) × (q₁q₂/r²) = (1/4πε) × (q₁q₂/r²) — in medium of dielectric constant K
  • 1/4πε₀ = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C² (Coulomb's constant)
  • ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²/N·m² (permittivity of free space)
  • Force is along the line joining charges. Like charges repel, unlike attract.
  • Principle of Superposition: Net force on a charge = vector sum of individual forces from all other charges. Forces don't affect each other.

3. Electric Field

  • E = F/q₀ — force per unit positive test charge (q₀ → 0 to avoid disturbing source field)
  • Due to point charge: E = (1/4πε₀)(q/r²) — directed away from +q, toward −q
  • Electric field is a vector; use superposition to find net field from multiple charges

4. Electric Dipole

Dipole moment: p = q × 2l (direction: −q to +q)
  • Axial field (on axis, distance r from centre): E = (1/4πε₀)(2p/r³) — direction same as p
  • Equatorial field (on perpendicular bisector): E = (1/4πε₀)(p/r³) — direction opposite to p
  • At same distance: E_axial = 2 × E_equatorial
  • Both vary as 1/r³ (unlike point charge which varies as 1/r²) — KEY EXAM POINT

5. Electric Field Lines — Rules

  • Start on +ve charge, end on −ve charge (or go to infinity)
  • Never intersect (field has unique direction at any point)
  • Perpendicular to conductor surface at every point
  • Denser field lines = stronger field
  • No closed loops for electrostatic field (conservative field)

6. Electric Flux and Gauss's Law

Φ = E · A = EA cosθ (for uniform field through flat surface)
Gauss's Law: Φ_total = q_enclosed / ε₀
  • Flux depends only on enclosed charge, NOT on shape/size of Gaussian surface
  • Charges outside the surface contribute ZERO net flux

▼

1. Electric Potential (V)

  • Work done per unit positive charge to bring a test charge from infinity to that point: V = W/q₀
  • Scalar quantity — add algebraically (no direction needed)
  • Potential due to point charge: V = (1/4πε₀)(q/r); negative for −q
  • Relation with field: E = −dV/dr (field = negative potential gradient)
  • For uniform field: V = −E·d

2. Equipotential Surfaces

  • Surface on which potential is same everywhere — no work done moving charge along it
  • Always perpendicular to electric field lines
  • For point charge: concentric spheres; for uniform field: parallel planes
  • Equipotential surfaces cannot intersect each other

3. Electrostatic Potential Energy

U = (1/4πε₀)(q₁q₂/r) — two-charge system
For n charges: U = Σ (1/4πε₀)(qᵢqⱼ/rᵢⱼ) — sum over all pairs
  • U > 0 (positive): like charges — stored energy, unstable if released
  • U < 0 (negative): unlike charges — bound state

4. Capacitance and Capacitors

C = Q/V (ratio of charge to potential difference)
Capacitor TypeCapacitance FormulaNote
Parallel plate (no dielectric)C = ε₀A/dA = area, d = separation
Parallel plate (with dielectric K)C = Kε₀A/d = KC₀K always ≥ 1
Spherical (isolated)C = 4πε₀RR = radius
Cylindrical (length L)C = 2πε₀L/ln(b/a)a,b = inner/outer radii

5. Dielectrics — Critical Concept

  • Dielectric constant K (or ε_r): dimensionless, always ≥ 1
  • Inserting dielectric increases capacitance by factor K
  • With battery connected (V = constant): Q increases (Q = CV), E stays constant (V/d), energy increases (U = ½CV²)
  • Disconnected from battery (Q = constant): V decreases (V = Q/C), E decreases, energy decreases (dielectric gets pulled in — energy released)

6. Combination of Capacitors

Series
1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...
  • Charge (Q) is SAME on each
  • Voltage adds: V = V₁ + V₂
  • C_eq < smallest capacitor
Parallel
C_eq = C₁ + C₂ + ...
  • Voltage (V) is SAME across each
  • Charge adds: Q = Q₁ + Q₂
  • C_eq > largest capacitor

7. Energy Stored in Capacitor

U = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/2C — all three forms equivalent
Energy density (per unit volume): u = ½ε₀E²
⚠️ Must-Know Distinction:
• Potential V (Volts) ≠ Potential Energy U (Joules)
• V = W/q; U = qV = work done
• When dielectric inserted with battery: energy INCREASES (battery does extra work)
• When dielectric inserted without battery: energy DECREASES (used to polarize dielectric)

▼

1. Drift Velocity and Current

  • Free electrons in conductor are in random thermal motion (no net current)
  • On applying field E: electrons drift opposite to E with average velocity v_d
  • I = nAev_d — where n = free electron density (electrons/m³), A = cross-section, e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C
  • Mobility μ = v_d/E = eτ/m (τ = relaxation time, m = electron mass)
  • v_d is very small (~10⁻⁴ m/s) but electrons move at ~10⁶ m/s thermally

2. Ohm's Law and Resistance

V = IR (Ohm's Law — valid for ohmic conductors)
R = ρL/A — ρ = resistivity (Ω·m), L = length, A = area
  • Resistivity depends on material and temperature, NOT on dimensions
  • Conductivity σ = 1/ρ
  • Ohm's law NOT valid for: diodes, electrolytes, gas discharge tubes (non-ohmic)

3. Temperature Dependence of Resistivity

ρ_T = ρ₀(1 + αΔT)
MaterialTemperature Coefficient αReason
Conductors (metals)Positive (α > 0)More collisions at higher T; resistance increases
Semiconductors (Si, Ge)Negative (α < 0)More carriers at higher T; resistance decreases
Alloys (Nichrome, Manganin)Very small (α ≈ 0)Nearly constant resistance — used in standards

4. Kirchhoff's Laws

KCL — Junction Rule
ΣI = 0 at any junction
  • Based on conservation of charge
  • Sum of currents entering = sum leaving
KVL — Loop Rule
ΣIR + ΣE = 0 around any loop
  • Based on conservation of energy
  • Algebraic sum of all potential drops = 0

5. EMF vs Terminal Voltage

  • EMF (ε): work done per unit charge by battery (open circuit voltage)
  • Internal resistance r: resistance of the battery itself
  • Terminal voltage during discharge: V = ε − Ir (less than EMF)
  • Terminal voltage during charging: V = ε + Ir (more than EMF)

6. Wheatstone Bridge

Balance condition: P/Q = R/S (no current through galvanometer)
  • Meter bridge: P/Q = l/(100−l) where l = balancing length
  • Unknown resistance: X = R(100−l)/l

7. Potentiometer

  • Works on principle: potential drop ∝ length of wire
  • Comparing EMFs: E₁/E₂ = l₁/l₂
  • Internal resistance: r = R(l₁/l₂ − 1)
  • Advantage over voltmeter: draws no current — measures true EMF
⚠️ Exam Traps:
• Carbon resistor color code: BB ROY of Great Britain has Very Good Wife (Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Grey, White = 0–9)
• Series resistors: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ (larger); Parallel: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ (smaller than smallest)
• Semiconductor conductivity increases with temperature (opposite to metals)

📖 Detailed Notes / विस्तृत नोट्स
▼

1. Magnetic Force — Lorentz Force

F = q(v × B) = qvB sinθ
  • Direction: use right-hand rule for v×B (for +ve charge); reverse for −ve charge
  • Magnetic force is always perpendicular to velocity → does NO work → doesn't change KE or speed
  • F = 0 when charge moves parallel to B (θ = 0°)
  • F = qvB (maximum) when perpendicular (θ = 90°)

2. Motion in Magnetic Field

  • v ⊥ B: Circular motion. Radius r = mv/qB; Period T = 2πm/qB (independent of speed!)
  • v ∥ B: No force, straight-line motion unchanged
  • v at angle θ: Helical path — circular (perpendicular component) + linear (parallel component)

3. Cyclotron

  • Uses perpendicular magnetic field to give charged particles circular paths
  • AC electric field (at cyclotron frequency) accelerates particles each half-cycle
  • Cyclotron frequency: f = qB/2πm — independent of speed (key principle!)
  • Limitation: cannot accelerate electrons (relativistic at low energy), cannot accelerate neutral particles
  • Maximum KE: KE_max = q²B²R²/2m (R = radius of dee)

4. Force on Current-Carrying Conductor

F = IL × B = BIL sinθ
  • Direction from Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (thumb = force, index = field, middle = current)
  • Force between two parallel wires: F/L = μ₀I₁I₂/2πd
  • Same direction currents: ATTRACTIVE; Opposite: REPULSIVE
  • Definition of 1 Ampere: force of 2×10⁻⁷ N/m between parallel wires 1 m apart

7. Torque on Current Loop and Galvanometer

τ = nBIA sinθ = MB sinθ (M = nIA = magnetic dipole moment)
  • Moving coil galvanometer: spring torque balances magnetic torque → θ ∝ I
  • Converted to ammeter: add low resistance shunt in parallel
  • Converted to voltmeter: add high resistance in series
⚠️ Common Mistakes:
• Right-hand rule: curl fingers in direction of current flow in loop → thumb points in direction of B (at centre)
• Cyclotron frequency is independent of particle speed — this is the KEY principle
• Magnetic force does NO work (always perpendicular to displacement)

1. Magnetic Dipole — Bar Magnet

  • Bar magnet is a magnetic dipole with moment M = m × 2l (m = pole strength, 2l = length)
  • Axial field (on axis): B = (μ₀/4π)(2M/r³) — in direction of M
  • Equatorial field (perpendicular bisector): B = (μ₀/4π)(M/r³) — opposite to M
  • At same distance: B_axial = 2 × B_equatorial

2. Torque and Energy of Magnetic Dipole

Torque: τ = MB sinθ (tends to align dipole with field)
Potential energy: U = −MB cosθ
  • Stable equilibrium: θ = 0° (M parallel to B), U = −MB (minimum)
  • Unstable equilibrium: θ = 180°, U = +MB (maximum)

5. Hysteresis and Applications

  • Hysteresis loop area = energy lost per cycle (as heat) in the material
  • Soft magnetic materials (small loop, low retentivity): Fe-Si — used in transformer cores, electromagnets (easy to magnetize/demagnetize)
  • Hard magnetic materials (wide loop, high retentivity, high coercivity): Steel — used in permanent magnets
  • Retentivity = B when H = 0 (residual magnetism)
  • Coercivity = H needed to reduce B to zero (demagnetizing field)
⚠️ Key Points:
• Curie temperature for Fe ≈ 1043 K — above this, ferromagnetic becomes paramagnetic
• Superconductors are perfect diamagnets (χ_m = −1, μ_r = 0) — Meissner effect
• Earth's geographic north is near magnetic south (compass north → magnetic south)

📖 Detailed Notes / विस्तृत नोट्स
▼

1. Faraday's Laws of EMI

  • First Law: Whenever magnetic flux through a circuit changes, an EMF is induced
  • Second Law: Induced EMF = −dΦ_B/dt (rate of change of flux); magnitude = |ΔΦ/Δt|
  • Magnetic flux: Φ_B = BA cosθ (B = field, A = area, θ = angle between B and normal to surface)
  • Flux can change by: changing B, changing A (deforming loop), changing θ (rotating loop)

2. Lenz's Law

  • Direction of induced current opposes the cause that produces it (consequence of energy conservation)
  • If flux is increasing → induced current creates field opposing B (reduces flux)
  • If flux is decreasing → induced current creates field supporting B (increases flux)
  • This is why there's a braking force on moving conductors in a field

3. Motional EMF

E = Blv sinθ — conductor of length l moving at speed v in field B
Rotating rod about one end: E = ½Bωl²
  • Free electrons experience Lorentz force F = qvB → charge separation → EMF
  • For a complete circuit: I = Blv/R (R = circuit resistance)
  • Power needed to maintain velocity: P = B²l²v²/R

4. Self-Inductance (L)

Φ_B = LI → Self-induced EMF: E = −L(dI/dt)
  • L depends on geometry only (shape, turns, core material)
  • Solenoid: L = μ₀n²Al = μ₀N²A/l (n = turns/length, N = total turns)
  • Toroid: L = μ₀N²A/2πr
  • Unit: Henry (H) = Ω·s
  • L opposes change in current — acts like "electrical inertia"

5. Mutual Inductance (M)

M = μ₀n₁n₂Al (for two coaxial solenoids)
EMF induced in coil 2: E₂ = −M(dI₁/dt)
  • M depends on geometry and relative orientation of the two coils
  • M is symmetric: M₁₂ = M₂₁
  • Transformer works on mutual induction principle

6. Eddy Currents

  • Induced currents in bulk conductors due to changing magnetic flux
  • Cause heating (I²R loss) — harmful in transformer cores → use laminated cores
  • Useful applications: induction heating, magnetic braking, speedometers, induction cooktops

7. Energy in Magnetic Field

U = ½LI² (energy stored in inductor)
Magnetic energy density: u_B = B²/2μ₀
⚠️ Important Analogies:
• Capacitor ↔ Inductor: Q ↔ I, V ↔ EMF, C ↔ L, U = ½CV² ↔ U = ½LI²
• In LC oscillation: energy oscillates between electric (capacitor) and magnetic (inductor)

  • If X_L < X_C: capacitive circuit, I leads V
  • If X_L = X_C: resonance, Z = R (minimum), φ = 0, I is maximum

4. Resonance

At resonance: ω₀ = 1/√(LC) → f₀ = 1/(2π√LC)
  • Z = R (minimum) → I = V/R (maximum)
  • Power factor cos φ = 1 (unity)
  • V_L = V_C (equal and opposite, they cancel); V_R = V (source)
  • V_L and V_C can individually be much larger than source voltage (resonance amplification)

5. Q-Factor (Quality Factor)

Q = ω₀L/R = 1/(ω₀CR) = (1/R)√(L/C)
  • Higher Q → sharper resonance peak, smaller bandwidth
  • Bandwidth Δω = ω₀/Q = R/L
  • Q also = V_L/V or V_C/V at resonance (voltage amplification factor)

6. Power in AC Circuits

P = V_rms × I_rms × cosφ = I²_rms × R
  • Power factor cosφ: ranges from 0 (pure L or C) to 1 (pure R or resonance)
  • Wattless current: component of current 90° out of phase with V — does no work
  • Apparent power (VA) = V_rms × I_rms; True power (W) = V_rms × I_rms × cosφ
  • Reactive power (VAR) = V_rms × I_rms × sinφ

7. Transformers

V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p = I_p/I_s (ideal transformer)
  • Step-up: N_s > N_p → V_s > V_p, but I_s < I_p
  • Step-down: N_s < N_p → V_s < V_p, but I_s > I_p
  • Ideal transformer: P_input = P_output (100% efficiency)
  • Losses in real transformer: copper loss (I²R), iron loss (eddy currents + hysteresis)
  • High-voltage transmission reduces I → reduces I²R power loss in transmission lines

  • Problem: Ampere's law failed when applied to a capacitor being charged (no real current between plates)

  • Total current = conduction current (I) + displacement current (I_D)

3. Electromagnetic Waves — Properties

  • Transverse waves: E and B oscillate perpendicular to each other AND to direction of propagation
  • E, B, and direction of travel form a mutually perpendicular triad (right-hand set)
  • E₀/B₀ = c (ratio of amplitudes equals speed of light)
  • Speed in vacuum: c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3×10⁸ m/s
  • Speed in medium: v = c/n (n = refractive index of medium)
  • E and B are in phase (both reach maximum and minimum at same time and place)

4. Energy in EM Waves

u_E = ½ε₀E² and u_B = B²/2μ₀ are ALWAYS EQUAL in EM waves
Total energy density: u = ε₀E² = B²/μ₀
Intensity (Poynting vector average): I = ½cε₀E₀² = P/A
  • Radiation pressure: P = I/c (absorption); P = 2I/c (perfect reflection)
  • EM waves carry momentum: p = U/c (for absorbed); p = 2U/c (for reflected)

5. Generation of EM Waves

  • Accelerating charges produce EM waves (oscillating dipole antenna)
  • Oscillating LC circuit: natural frequency f = 1/(2π√LC)
  • EM waves are self-sustaining: changing E produces B, changing B produces E
⚠️ Key Facts to Memorize:
• All EM waves travel at same speed c in vacuum regardless of frequency
• All EM waves are transverse; sound is longitudinal (NOT EM)
• u_E = u_B always — unique property of EM waves
• EM spectrum: radio → micro → IR → visible → UV → X-ray → gamma (increasing frequency/energy, decreasing wavelength)

1. Huygens' Principle

  • Every point on a wavefront acts as a secondary source of spherical wavelets
  • New wavefront = envelope of all secondary wavelets after time t
  • Explains reflection, refraction, and diffraction geometrically
  • Types of wavefronts: Spherical (point source), Cylindrical (line source), Plane (source at infinity)

2. Interference — Young's Double Slit

  • Coherent sources: same frequency, constant phase difference (must use same source)
  • Path difference Δ = d sinθ ≈ yd/D (y = fringe position, D = screen distance, d = slit separation)
  • Constructive: Δ = nλ → bright fringes
  • Destructive: Δ = (n + ½)λ → dark fringes
Fringe width β = λD/d — spacing between consecutive bright (or dark) fringes
  • β increases if: λ increases, D increases, d decreases
  • If entire setup is immersed in medium n: λ_medium = λ/n → β decreases by factor n
  • Intensity: I = 4I₀ cos²(δ/2) where δ = phase difference = (2π/λ)Δ

3. Single Slit Diffraction

Minima: a sinθ = nλ (n = 1, 2, 3...) where a = slit width
  • Width of central maximum = 2λD/a (twice the secondary maxima width)
  • Angular width of central maximum = 2λ/a
  • Secondary maxima at: a sinθ = (2n+1)λ/2 (approximately)
  • Intensity of secondary maxima decreases rapidly (1st = 4.5% of central)
  • Narrower slit → wider diffraction pattern (Δ ∝ 1/a)

• Minimum wavelength (series limit) = maximum energy transition (to ∞)

▼

1. Nuclear Composition & Size

  • Nucleus = Protons (Z) + Neutrons (N); Mass number A = Z + N
  • Isotopes: same Z, different A (e.g., H-1, H-2, H-3); Isobars: same A; Isotones: same N
  • Atomic mass unit: 1 u = 1.66054 × 10⁻²⁷ kg = 931.5 MeV/c²
  • Nuclear radius: R = R₀A^(1/3), R₀ = 1.2 fm (1 fm = 10⁻¹⁵ m)
  • Nuclear density ≈ 2.3 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³ — same for all nuclei (constant!)

2. Mass Defect & Binding Energy

Mass defect: Δm = [Z·m_p + (A−Z)·m_n] − m_nucleus
Binding energy: BE = Δm × c² = Δm × 931.5 MeV (if Δm in u)
  • BE per nucleon = BE/A — measures nuclear stability
  • Higher BE/A → more stable nucleus
  • Fe-56 has maximum BE/A ≈ 8.8 MeV — most stable nucleus
  • Light nuclei (A < 56): fusion releases energy (BE/A increases)
  • Heavy nuclei (A > 56): fission releases energy (BE/A increases for products)

3. Nuclear Forces

  • Strongest of four fundamental forces; only acts over very short range (< 2–3 fm)
  • Charge-independent: n-n, p-p, n-p forces are all equal
  • Attractive for r > 0.8 fm; repulsive for r < 0.8 fm (prevents collapse)
  • Spin-dependent: parallel spins give stronger binding
  • Non-central: not always along line joining nucleons

5. Radioactive Decay Law

N(t) = N₀ e^(−λt)  |  Activity: A = λN = A₀ e^(−λt)
Half-life: T₁/₂ = 0.693/λ  |  Mean life: τ = 1/λ = 1.44 × T₁/₂
  • After n half-lives: N = N₀/2ⁿ; Activity = A₀/2ⁿ
  • τ (mean life) > T₁/₂ always (mean life is 44% longer than half-life)
  • Decay is statistical — individual decay is random; law describes average behavior
  • SI unit of activity: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay/second; 1 Curie = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq

6. Fission & Fusion

Nuclear Fission
²³⁵₉₂U + n → ⁹⁴₃₆Kr + ¹³⁹₅₆Ba + 3n + ~200 MeV
  • Heavy nucleus splits into two medium-mass nuclei
  • Releases neutrons → chain reaction possible
  • Critical mass needed for self-sustained reaction
  • Used in nuclear reactors and atomic bombs
Nuclear Fusion
²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + n + 17.6 MeV
  • Light nuclei combine to form heavier nucleus
  • Requires very high temperature (~10⁷ K) — thermonuclear
  • Source of energy in stars (Sun)
  • More energy per unit mass than fission

• 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c² — use this for BE calculations
• Mass of proton = 1.007276 u; neutron = 1.008665 u
• Q-value > 0: exothermic (energy released); Q < 0: endothermic (energy absorbed)
• In nuclear reactions: Charge (Z) and Mass number (A) are both conserved

1. Energy Band Theory

MaterialBand Gap (E_g)ConductivityExample

  • In semiconductors at room temp: some electrons cross the gap → conductivity
  • Higher temperature → more electron-hole pairs → higher conductivity (opposite to metals)
  • Holes: positive charge carriers (absence of electron in valence band)

2. Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Semiconductors

n-type (pentavalent dopant)
  • Dopants: P, As, Sb (5 valence electrons)
  • Extra electron donated → majority carriers = electrons
  • Minority carriers = holes
  • Fermi level shifts toward conduction band
p-type (trivalent dopant)
  • Dopants: B, Al, Ga, In (3 valence electrons)
  • Electron accepted → creates hole → majority carriers = holes
  • Minority carriers = electrons
  • Fermi level shifts toward valence band

3. P-N Junction Diode

  • Depletion region: formed at junction; has no free carriers; creates built-in potential barrier (~0.3 V Ge, ~0.7 V Si)
  • Forward bias: p connected to +ve terminal; barrier reduced; current flows; threshold voltage ~0.7 V (Si)
  • Reverse bias: p connected to −ve terminal; barrier increased; negligible current (only minority carriers) until breakdown
  • Zener diode: works in controlled reverse breakdown; used as voltage regulator (V_Z = constant)

6. Transistor — Action & Configurations

  • Three regions: Emitter (heavily doped), Base (thin, lightly doped), Collector (moderately doped)
  • Transistor action: base must be very thin so most injected carriers diffuse to collector
  • α = I_C/I_E (always < 1); β = I_C/I_B (typically 20–200)
  • Relation: I_E = I_B + I_C; β = α/(1−α); α = β/(1+β)
  • CE configuration: most commonly used; provides current, voltage, and power gain

7. Transistor as Amplifier (CE Mode)

Voltage gain A_V = β × (R_C/R_i)  |  Power gain A_P = β² × (R_C/R_i)
  • Output is 180° out of phase with input in CE mode
  • Transconductance g_m = ΔI_C/ΔV_BE = β/r_i
